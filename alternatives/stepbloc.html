When should I pick StepBloc instead?

Honest answer — pick StepBloc if any of these matter more than ambient activity, Apple Watch, and the lower lifetime price:

  • You actively want to do push-ups or squats to unlock — the indoor rep mechanic is a genuinely different design choice and StepBloc owns it. EarnLock does not count reps; it counts continuous movement.
  • You need the app on iPad, Mac (Apple Silicon), or Apple Vision Pro — StepBloc ships those platforms; EarnLock is iPhone + Apple Watch only.
  • You prefer Japanese, Simplified Chinese, or Spanish localizations that have been tuned for those markets specifically.

EarnLock is built for the person who tried StepBloc, did the 10 push-ups on the couch in 20 seconds, and realized the friction needed to be ambient — paid in steps spread across the day, not in reps fired off in a burst.

FAQ

What is the best alternative to StepBloc?
EarnLock is the closest direct alternative to StepBloc among screen-time blockers that gate access on physical activity. The differences are mechanic, surface, and presentation. EarnLock uses steps, active minutes, and active calories — continuous, ambient, hard to game indoors. StepBloc adds push-ups and squats — rep-based exercises you can complete sitting in front of the same couch you were trying to leave. EarnLock has a first-class Apple Watch app and complications; StepBloc has no Watch app listed. EarnLock's lifetime tier is $19.99; StepBloc's is $24.99.
How is EarnLock different from StepBloc?
Four things. (1) Unlock mechanic: EarnLock uses ambient body movement (steps / active minutes / active calories) read from Apple Health; StepBloc adds rep counting for push-ups and squats. We picked ambient over reps deliberately — reps are gameable at home; steps and active-minutes require continuous motion. (2) Apple Watch: EarnLock ships a first-class Watch app and complications; StepBloc has no Watch app listed. (3) Pricing: EarnLock's lifetime is $19.99 (one-time); StepBloc's lifetime is $24.99. (4) Privacy and reach: EarnLock's App Privacy label reads Data Not Collected and the app ships in 39 locales; StepBloc collects Health, Fitness, Usage Data, and Diagnostics under Data Not Linked, and ships in 3 locales.
Is EarnLock cheaper than StepBloc?
Yes. EarnLock's $19.99 one-time lifetime tier undercuts StepBloc's $24.99 lifetime by exactly $5. EarnLock's monthly is $1.99 versus StepBloc's $3.99 — half. EarnLock's yearly is $9.99 (Family Sharing eligible across 5 family members), with both monthly and yearly carrying a 7-day free trial. Both apps ship a free baseline tier.
Why does EarnLock use steps instead of push-ups and squats?
Because reps are gameable and steps are continuous. You can do 10 push-ups in 20 seconds without standing up from the couch you were trying to leave. You cannot fake 8,000 steps in a single burst — the count requires sustained, distributed motion measured by Apple Health, and it accrues across the day's actual movement. EarnLock's premise is that the unlock cost should be denominated in something the user's life also benefits from, and ambient activity does that better than indoor reps. Both apps cite Apple Health for steps; EarnLock additionally supports active minutes and active calories in Premium.
Does StepBloc have an Apple Watch app?
Per StepBloc's App Store listing (developer Hamsters Technology, app ID 6480580480), the app runs on iPhone, iPad, Mac (M1+), and visionOS — no Apple Watch app is listed. EarnLock ships a first-class watchOS app: the Watch reads HealthKit directly so the count keeps running during long walks, runs, and gym sessions without the iPhone in your pocket. Complications mount on modular, circular, corner, and graphic faces.
Which is more private — EarnLock or StepBloc?
EarnLock's Apple App Privacy label reads Data Not Collected — no analytics SDK, no advertising SDK, no third-party SDKs of any kind, no EarnLock server. StepBloc's App Privacy label lists Health, Fitness, Usage Data (Product Interaction, Other Usage Data), and Diagnostics (Crash Data) under Data Not Linked. Neither app tracks you across other apps. EarnLock is the stricter zero-collection design.
