It's the Saturday before Easter. For a lot of people in the northern hemisphere this weekend has the structural shape of a pause — even if, like me, you're not religious, the calendar treats this as a hinge. The week is over. The next week hasn't started. There's a window.

This is a short post about a small thing the studio makes for that window. [Zen 2048](/apps/zen-2048/), as the name implies, is a 2048 puzzle that does not try to ruin your day.

## The 2048 problem

The original 2048, by Gabriele Cirulli, was open-sourced in 2014 and became one of the most-cloned games in mobile history. The math is elegant: combine matching tiles, reach 2048 (or 4096, or 8192). The form is perfect for short, contemplative play — the kind of thing that fits a coffee shop queue or a five-minute window.

What the App Store did to 2048 over the next decade was less elegant. The clones added:

- **Pre-roll video ads** before each new game.
- **Interstitial ads** every few moves.
- **Lives**, refilled by watching ads or paying.
- **Daily login bonuses** engineered to drive retention.
- **Leaderboards** ranked against bots, designed to bait competitive scrolling.
- **"Earn rewards" loops** — coins, spins, prize framing — stapled onto a number puzzle.
- **Save-state hostage-taking** — your high score and progress live in the cloud, behind an account.

The puzzle itself, the actual game, was buried under a layer of monetisation infrastructure that had nothing to do with combining numbered tiles. By 2026 it was almost impossible to find a clean version on the iOS App Store. The original web version still works in a phone browser, but it's clunky and there's no real offline mode.

Zen 2048 exists to put the original game back where it belongs — and then to make it feel like something.

## What does Zen 2048 specifically do?

The first half of the answer is a list of what it *doesn't* do, which is most of the list above:

- No ads.
- No lives.
- No "watch a video to continue."
- No daily-login coercion.
- No leaderboards ranked against bots.
- No account.
- No cloud sync of your scores by default.
- No tracking — the App Store privacy label declares no data collected, because nothing is.
- No micro-transaction wall in front of the game.

The second half is what it adds, because removing the junk wasn't the whole ambition. Zen 2048 rebuilds the board in **real 3D** — you can tilt the phone or drag to spin the board to any angle and watch the tiles catch the light, then it snaps back to a clean top-down view the moment you swipe. Every tile is a **material** rather than a flat colour, drawn from 17 designer themes across seven families: glass, ceramic, paper, metal, neon, pastel, monochrome. There's a quiet daily challenge and a streak that rewards coming back without guilt-tripping you for a missed day. The haptics are tuned to make a merge feel like something, not to buzz at you constantly.

And it's **free**. That word does a lot of work on the App Store, usually meaning "free because you are the product." Here it just means free: the whole puzzle, the 3D board, the daily challenge, and the free Classic theme cost nothing, with nothing watching you. The app funds itself the honest way — optional designer **theme packs** ($1.99–$7.99) and optional **undo tokens** ($0.99–$6.99) for players who want a safety net. Neither is ever required, neither is dangled mid-game, and neither changes whether you can win. If you never spend a cent, you have the complete game.

That's the line I care about. The problem with 2048 clones was never that someone wanted to get paid. It's that the payment mechanisms — ads, lives, retention bait — were hostile to the act of playing. A one-time theme you buy because you like how amber glass looks is not a dark pattern. A pre-roll video before every game is.

## Why a small game still matters

The argument for building Zen 2048 in 2026 is that *small calm things still have a place on the phone*, and the App Store has not been making it easy to find them.

Most of the discoverability mechanics on the App Store reward apps that game retention metrics. The recommendation algorithm prefers apps that increase daily active users. The featuring algorithms prefer apps with high engagement curves. None of those incentives align with a small puzzle that the user opens for five minutes when they want quiet.

A small puzzle that respects your attention is the kind of thing that has to be recommended by a friend, found through a journal post, or installed by a user who has explicitly gone looking for the alternative. That's a slow distribution model. It is also, I think, the only honest one for software in this shape.

## A small offering for the long weekend

If you have a window this weekend — between meals, after the family obligations, in the quiet hour before bed — and you're looking for something small and finite to do with your hands and your attention, [Zen 2048](/apps/zen-2048/) is the studio's tiniest offering. The puzzle, in 3D, no ads, no lives, no dark patterns. Free to play, and yours to make beautiful if you want to.

If you don't, that's fine too. The post is short on purpose. Go for a walk.

Happy long weekend.
